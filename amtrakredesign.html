Intro
The project is a redesign of Amtrak's website. We wanted to redesign the process of purchasing a train ticket that would lead to better user satisfaction. The challenge was choosing one simple component, or one step in purchasing a train ticket, to redesign that would yield the best results in leading to better user satisfaction. Wouldnâ€™t it be better to select round trip tickets without a hassle?

The project was designed along with 3 other students in COGS 187a, a UX course at UC San Diego. It was part of a design sprint and played as our final. My role in the project was to conduct user testing, analyze and gain insight from user testing, create digital mockups, and create a final design rationale.
Process
User Testing
To understand the context in which our system would be used, our team conducted 9 interviews on users mainly aged 18-25 who regularly listen to music on their mobile device. We discovered the most used features in music applications - create playlist , search bar, music library, and download playlist - and the least used features - homepage, radio, and browse. Furthermore, we learned that Spotify dominated the market and therefore we decided to use it as a basis of convention. From this analysis, we constructed 10 common scenarios experience when using these applications.
Process
User interviews
To understand how users purchased train tickets, our team observed 3 users perform seven tasks while they simultaneously performed think-alouds. We followed the observations with interview questions about the users' experiences. From the observations and interviews we discovered that our users encountered a problem related to the overwhelming amount of information presented to them when they were looking through different routes for train tickets.
Design problem
After completing user research, we reframed our design problem as: "How to make the route results page show all the route information in a manner that doesn't overwhelm the user?". In other words, our redesign focuses on making the route results page show all the route information in a more concise and organized manner so that the user does not have to scroll down to see more options or get confused in the process of purchasing their ticket.
Ideation
In order to find efficient and effective workflow, our team created 2 distinct redesigns that had distinct solutions in solving the design problem.

Our first redesign focuses on maintaining the functionality of Amtrak while reducing the cognitive workload on the user. When reviewing Amtrak's current design, we noticed that the following as influencers to the cognitive overload: lack of distinguishing ticket options, lack of minimalist design, and the grand scope each ticket option took. To help the user distinguish ticket options apart, we decided to increase the spacing between the blocks and make each block stand out with a white background against the site's grey background. To make the design minimal, we decided to get rid of the journey option on the top and the available information in the header as we thought these dialogues contained irrelevant or rarely needed information. We used this design decision to prompt the design decision of including the train route information in the search bar. This decision gives the user control, freedom, and flexibility in changing their train routes. Lastly, to minimize the user's memory load, we compressed the train route information into a scope that was easy for the user to analyze and process. We hid the transfers information under the itinerary and put the ticket options and prices into one side of the screen.

Our second redesign focuses on condensing each train option into one element (or row) on the page. Originally, one option for a train would encompass the entire web page. Now, the user can see the total amount of time, destination, number of transfers, and a separate full itinerary all within eye level. The user can also click through the different types of tickets and its prices on the drop down, then easily add it to the cart. The overall goal is to condense and reorganize the most information presented to the users trying to purchase a ticket. Not only does it minimize the confusion and workload for the users to understand, it still provides the required information needed for a user to make a complete decision.
Prototype
After ideating and listing potential solutions, I, as the head UX and UI designer of the group, used figma to make a digital mockup of the first redesign. My team used InVision to add interactivity to the prototype.
Conclusion
Prototype Testing and rationale
After having the original test users perform tasks on the first redesign prototype and the original Amtrak website and answer interview questions, we found that the first redesign did prove to work better than the original Amtrak website.

Regarding our first redesign, our approach to helping the user distinguish ticket options apart did lead to better user satisfaction. As seen in user interviews, users said that the price selection was easier overall in the redesign than in the original Amtrak website. This was due to the color gradient acting as a means of recognition rather than recall. Furthermore, our approach to making the design minimal did lead to better user satisfaction as it helped the users navigate each page with less hassle. Lastly, our approach to compressing the scope to minimize the user's memory load did lead to better user satisfaction. As seen in our interviews, all three users in our second user testing round mentioned that the stops were easier to see in the first redesign prototype because it was simple and displayed only the immediately relevant information. They stated that the original Amtrak website had too much information as it listed out each transfer stop explicitly, making the page too cluttered.
Lessons learned
Unfortunately we were not able to conduct user testing on our second redesign and therefore unable to validate our design decisions. If we had more time to complete the project, we would have conducted testing on our second redesign. Additionally, we would have conducted more testing to validate the impact of our design solution. Lastly, we would have done a few more iterations of ideation to produce a better and more data-driven design solution.
Final thoughts
Overall, the project gave me a lot of insight on rapid prototyping. As part of our final for our class, the project was meant to challenge us to define and solve the design problem of a random topic of our choosing. From making a user test plan to creating fast prototypes of two design solutions, I was able gain experience with a design sprint.
